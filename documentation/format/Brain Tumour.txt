INTRODUCTION
1.1 Introduction
The human brain is one of the most complex and significant organs in the human body. It controls all voluntary and involuntary activities, including thinking, memory, emotions, movement, and coordination. The proper functioning of the brain is essential for human survival and daily activities. Any abnormality occurring in the brain can seriously affect the nervous system and overall body functioning. Among the various neurological disorders, brain tumors are considered one of the most dangerous and life-threatening diseases.
A brain tumor [1] refers to the abnormal growth of cells inside the brain or surrounding tissues. Tumors may be classified as benign or malignant based on their growth behavior and impact on surrounding tissues. Benign tumors are non-cancerous and grow slowly, while malignant tumors are cancerous and spread rapidly to nearby tissues. Early identification and treatment of brain tumors are essential to prevent severe complications and improve the survival rate of patients.
Medical imaging plays a crucial role in the diagnosis and analysis of brain tumors. Among various medical imaging techniques, Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)[4] is widely used because it provides detailed images of soft tissues in the brain without exposing patients to harmful radiation. MRI images help radiologists identify the size, location, and type of tumors effectively.
Traditionally, radiologists manually examine MRI scans [3] to diagnose tumors. However, manual diagnosis is time-consuming, complex, and highly dependent on the expertise of medical professionals. Due to variations in tumor shape, size, intensity, and location, accurate tumor identification becomes challenging. In some cases, human errors and delayed diagnosis may lead to severe consequences for patients.
Recent advancements in Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning (DL)[8] have transformed healthcare and medical image analysis. Deep learning algorithms can automatically learn hidden patterns from large datasets and perform intelligent classification with high accuracy. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), a specialized type of deep learning architecture, are highly effective in image processing and medical diagnosis applications.
The proposed work introduces an Intelligent Neural Classification Strategy (INCS) for brain tumor detection using MRI images[3]. The system combines image preprocessing, segmentation, feature extraction, and deep learning classification techniques to identify tumors accurately. The proposed model aims to assist radiologists and healthcare professionals in early diagnosis and treatment planning.
The study mainly focuses on detecting different types of brain tumors such as glioma, meningioma, and pituitary tumors using intelligent deep learning[9] techniques. The proposed methodology improves classification performance and reduces dependency on manual interpretation.

1.2 Background of the Problem
Brain tumors are among the major causes of neurological disorders and cancer-related deaths worldwide. A tumor develops when cells grow uncontrollably and form an abnormal mass inside the brain. These tumors can affect brain functionality by damaging surrounding tissues and increasing pressure inside the skull.
There are more than 120 types of brain tumors identified in medical science. Brain tumors are broadly classified into two categories:
1.2.1 Primary Brain Tumors
Primary brain tumors originate within the brain itself. They may develop from brain tissues[3], nerve cells, membranes, or glands. These tumors can be either benign or malignant.
1.2.2 Secondary Brain Tumors
Secondary or metastatic brain tumors occur when cancer spreads from other body parts to the brain. These tumors are generally malignant and more dangerous.
Brain tumors can produce symptoms such as:
Severe headaches 
Vision problems 
Memory loss 
Seizures 
Difficulty in speaking 
Loss of coordination 
Behavioral changes 
The complexity of brain structures makes tumor diagnosis a difficult task. MRI[6] is considered one of the best imaging techniques for diagnosing brain abnormalities because it provides high-resolution images with better tissue contrast.
However, manual analysis of MRI images presents several challenges:
Variations in tumor shape and size 
Similar appearance between healthy and abnormal tissues 
Time-consuming diagnosis 
Human interpretation errors 
Limited availability of experienced radiologists 
To overcome these limitations, researchers have introduced computer-aided diagnosis systems using machine learning and deep learning methods. Deep learning approaches automatically extract features from images and perform accurate classification without extensive manual intervention.
CNN[10]-based approaches have shown significant improvements in medical image analysis because they preserve spatial information and identify hidden patterns effectively. The proposed Intelligent Neural Classification Strategy (INCS) [17]is developed based on deep learning principles to improve tumor classification performance.

2.1 Introduction
Brain tumor detection[2] and classification have become one of the most important research areas in medical image processing and artificial intelligence. A brain tumor is an abnormal growth of cells inside the brain that affects the normal functioning of the central nervous system. Brain tumors can be benign or malignant depending on their growth characteristics and severity. Early and accurate diagnosis of tumors is essential for improving patient survival rates and treatment planning.

Medical imaging techniques play a major role in the diagnosis of neurological disorders. Among various imaging modalities such as CT scan, PET scan, and MRI, Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)[4] is widely used because it provides high-resolution images with better soft tissue contrast. MRI scans help radiologists identify tumor location, size, texture, and abnormalities in brain tissues.

Traditionally, brain tumor diagnosis depends on manual examination of MRI images[16] by radiologists. However, manual analysis is difficult, time-consuming, and prone to human errors. The complexity of brain structures and the variations in tumor appearance make accurate diagnosis challenging. Therefore, automated computer-aided diagnosis systems have gained significant attention in recent years.

Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning (DL) techniques have revolutionized the field of medical image analysis. Deep learning models, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)[20], have demonstrated excellent performance in image classification, segmentation, and feature extraction tasks. CNNs automatically learn meaningful spatial features from MRI images and improve classification accuracy without manual feature engineering.

Several researchers have proposed various approaches for brain tumor detection using machine learning algorithms, neural networks, transfer learning, segmentation techniques, and hybrid deep learning[13] models. Many systems achieved high accuracy in classification tasks; however, challenges such as overfitting, high computational complexity, lack of real-time implementation, and dependency on large datasets still remain unresolved.

2.5 Limitations of Existing Methods
Although significant advancements have been achieved in brain tumor detection and classification using machine learning and deep learning techniques[17], several limitations still exist in current systems. These limitations affect the overall performance, reliability, scalability, and practical implementation of automated medical diagnosis systems. The major limitations identified from the literature survey are discussed below.
2.5.1 Overfitting Problems
One of the major challenges in deep learning-based brain tumor detection[11] systems is overfitting. Overfitting[8] occurs when a model performs exceptionally well on training data but fails to generalize effectively on unseen or test data. This issue mainly arises because deep learning models learn unnecessary details and noise present in the training dataset instead of learning generalized patterns.
Medical image[7] datasets are often limited in size, and training complex neural networks on smaller datasets increases the probability of overfitting. As a result, the model may produce high training accuracy while showing poor performance in real-time diagnosis.
Overfitting reduces:
Model reliability 
Prediction consistency 
Generalization capability 
Clinical applicability 
To minimize overfitting, researchers apply techniques such as:
Data augmentation 
Dropout layers 
Regularization methods 
Cross-validation 
Transfer learning 
Despite these approaches, overfitting still remains a significant challenge in many existing systems.
2.5.2 Computational Complexity
Most advanced deep learning[9] architectures used for brain tumor classification are computationally intensive. Models such as ResNet, EfficientNet, DenseNet, and hybrid CNN architectures[12] contain millions of parameters that require extensive computational resources for training and testing.
The complexity of these models increases:
Memory utilization 
Processing time 
Energy consumption 
Hardware dependency 
Training deep learning models on large MRI datasets requires:
High-performance GPU systems 
Large storage capacity 
Parallel computing environments 
This computational burden makes implementation difficult in small hospitals, rural healthcare centers, and low-resource environments. High computational complexity[17] also affects real-time processing and increases deployment costs.
Therefore, designing lightweight and computationally efficient models remains an important research challenge in medical image[5] analysis.
2.5.3 Lack of Real-Time Systems
Many existing brain tumor detection systems [13] are developed primarily for research purposes and are not optimized for real-time clinical applications. Real-time diagnosis is essential in healthcare environments because delayed decisions may affect patient treatment and survival.
Several deep learning models require:
Longer execution time 
Multiple preprocessing stages 
Complex segmentation operations 
Large computational resources 
These factors reduce the speed of diagnosis and limit practical usability in emergency medical situations.
Real-time medical systems must provide:
Faster prediction 
Low latency 
High reliability 
Continuous performance 
However, most current systems focus mainly on classification accuracy while neglecting execution efficiency and response time. Hence, developing real-time automated diagnosis systems remains a major challenge in existing approaches.
2.5.4 Dependency on Dataset Size
Deep learning[9] models require large amounts of labeled training data for effective learning and accurate prediction. In medical image analysis[12], obtaining large annotated MRI datasets is difficult due to:
Privacy concerns 
Limited patient records 
High annotation cost 
Requirement of expert radiologists 
Small datasets negatively affect:
Model training 
Feature learning capability 
Classification accuracy 
Generalization performance 
Insufficient training data also increases the risk of overfitting and reduces model robustness. Additionally, medical datasets often suffer from class imbalance problems where certain tumor categories contain fewer samples compared to others.
Although transfer learning and data augmentation[16] techniques help improve performance on smaller datasets, dependency on large annotated datasets still remains one of the major limitations of deep learning-based medical diagnosis systems.
2.5.5 Difficulty in Accurate Segmentation
Tumor segmentation[18] is one of the most critical stages in brain tumor analysis. Accurate segmentation helps identify tumor boundaries, shape, size, and affected regions. However, segmentation remains highly challenging because brain tumors vary significantly in:
Shape 
Size 
Texture 
Intensity 
Location 
MRI images may also contain:
Noise 
Low contrast regions 
Intensity inhomogeneity 
Artifacts 
These factors make it difficult for segmentation algorithms to isolate tumor regions accurately.
Improper segmentation affects:
Feature extraction 
Classification accuracy 
Tumor localization 
Diagnostic performance 
Although advanced models such as U-Net[15] and attention-based segmentation networks improve performance, accurate tumor segmentation under varying conditions continues to be a major research issue.
2.5.6 Limited Explainability
Most deep learning[18] models function as black-box systems, meaning they provide predictions without clearly explaining how decisions are made. In healthcare applications, explainability is extremely important because doctors and radiologists must understand the reasoning behind predictions before making clinical decisions.
Lack of explainability creates several challenges such as:
Reduced trust in AI systems 
Difficulty in validating predictions 
Ethical concerns 
Limited clinical acceptance 
Medical professionals require systems that can provide:
Visual explanations 
Tumor localization maps 
Decision reasoning 
Confidence scores 
Although techniques such as Grad-CAM[20] and Explainable AI (XAI) have been introduced, many existing systems still lack sufficient interpretability and transparency.
Improving explainability while maintaining high classification accuracy remains an important challenge in AI-assisted medical diagnosis systems.

Training deep learning [13]models for brain tumor detection requires significant time due to:
Large MRI datasets

Models such as ResNet, DenseNet[7], and EfficientNet require extensive training iterations to achieve optimal performance. Training time further increases when:

Reducing training time while maintaining high classification accuracy is therefore a major challenge in deep learning[8]-based brain tumor detection systems.

The augmented images introduced additional variations into the training dataset without altering the underlying tumor characteristics. This enabled the model to learn more robust and generalized features. As a result, the classifier became less sensitive to image orientation, position, and scale variations.
Experimental analysis revealed that data augmentation significantly reduced overfitting. The validation accuracy improved noticeably after augmentation, and the gap between training and validation performance decreased. The model demonstrated better generalization capability when evaluated on unseen test images.
Data augmentation also increased the model's ability to classify tumors appearing in different positions and orientations within MRI images[1]. This improvement is particularly important for real-world clinical applications, where MRI scans may exhibit substantial variability.
Overall, data augmentation enhanced the robustness, reliability, and predictive capability of the proposed INCS model.

4.7.6 Comparison with Existing Systems
The proposed INCS model[5] was compared with several existing brain tumor detection approaches, including traditional machine learning methods, CNN-based classifiers, transfer learning models, and hybrid deep learning architectures.
Traditional machine learning algorithms such as Support Vector Machines (SVM) and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)[8] generally require manual feature extraction and exhibit lower classification accuracy. Although these methods are computationally simpler, they lack the ability to automatically learn complex spatial patterns from MRI images.
CNN-based models improved classification performance by introducing automatic feature extraction capabilities. However, many CNN architectures suffer from overfitting and require extensive hyperparameter tuning. Transfer learning models such as EfficientNet and ResNet[10] achieved excellent accuracy but often involve high computational complexity and large model sizes.
Compared to these approaches, the proposed INCS model provides a balanced solution by combining efficient preprocessing, accurate segmentation, automatic feature extraction, and intelligent classification. The achieved accuracy of 97.87% demonstrates competitive performance while maintaining lower complexity and improved interpretability.
The comparative analysis confirms that the proposed methodology effectively addresses several limitations of existing systems and provides reliable performance for brain tumor diagnosis.

The clinical significance of the proposed INCS model[8] lies in its ability to assist healthcare professionals in the early detection and classification of brain tumors. Accurate and timely diagnosis is essential for determining appropriate treatment strategies and improving patient outcomes.

The system also has the potential to support healthcare facilities in regions where experienced radiologists are limited. Automated diagnosis tools can help bridge the gap between increasing patient demands and available medical expertise.
By minimizing false negatives, the model reduces the risk of missed tumor diagnoses, while high specificity minimizes unnecessary medical interventions for healthy patients. These characteristics enhance patient safety and improve healthcare quality.
Furthermore, the proposed system can be integrated into hospital information systems, telemedicine platforms, and intelligent healthcare applications to support real-time medical diagnosis[5]. The model's ability to classify multiple tumor categories makes it suitable for practical clinical deployment and future healthcare innovations.

Brain tumor[2] detection and classification play a vital role in modern healthcare because early diagnosis significantly improves treatment planning and patient survival rates. Manual analysis of Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)[1] scans by radiologists is often time-consuming, complex, and susceptible to human error. Therefore, the development of intelligent automated diagnostic systems has become an important research area in medical image analysis.
This thesis presented an Intelligent Neural Classification Strategy (INCS) for automated brain tumor detection and classification using MRI images. The proposed methodology integrates image preprocessing, tumor segmentation, feature extraction, and deep learning-based classification into a unified framework. The system was designed to classify MRI images into four categories: glioma tumor, meningioma tumor, pituitary tumor, and normal brain tissue.
Initially, MRI images were subjected to preprocessing operations such as grayscale conversion, noise removal, normalization, resizing, and data augmentation. These operations improved image quality, reduced computational complexity, and enhanced the learning capability of the neural network. Tumor segmentation was then performed to isolate abnormal regions and identify tumor boundaries accurately. The segmented images were further processed through Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)[7] layers for automatic feature extraction and classification.
The proposed INCS[9] model successfully learned discriminative tumor features and demonstrated excellent classification capability. Experimental evaluation was conducted using a publicly available MRI dataset, and performance was measured using metrics such as Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-Score, Specificity, Confusion Matrix, and ROC Curve analysis.

These results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model in accurately identifying and classifying different categories of brain tumors.
The confusion matrix[14] analysis revealed that the majority of MRI images were correctly classified, with only a few misclassifications occurring between visually similar tumor classes. ROC curve analysis further confirmed the robustness of the model by achieving an Area Under Curve (AUC) value close to 0.98, indicating excellent classification performance.
Overall, the proposed Intelligent Neural Classification Strategy[18] provides an efficient, reliable, and intelligent framework for automated brain tumor diagnosis. The system reduces dependency on manual analysis, supports radiologists in clinical decision-making, and has the potential to improve healthcare services through accurate and timely tumor detection.

5.3 Limitations
Although the proposed Intelligent Neural Classification Strategy[6] achieved promising results, certain limitations remain that can be addressed in future research.

Dependence on Dataset Quality:
The performance of the model depends heavily on the quality and diversity of the MRI[1] dataset. Poor-quality images or limited datasets may reduce classification performance.

Computational Requirements:
Training deep learning models[3] requires considerable computational resources, including high-performance processors and GPU support.

Limited Real-Time Validation:
The proposed model was evaluated using offline datasets. Real-time deployment and validation in hospital environments were not performed during this study.

Segmentation Challenges:
Although segmentation performance was satisfactory, images containing low-contrast tumors and irregular tumor boundaries remain challenging for accurate localization.

Lack of Explainability:
Like many deep learning systems, the proposed model operates primarily as a black-box classifier and does not fully explain the reasoning behind its predictions.

Dataset Imbalance:
Certain tumor[2] categories may contain fewer training samples compared to others, which can affect classification consistency.


Clinical Validation Requirements:
The model requires further clinical testing and validation using large-scale hospital datasets before deployment in real-world healthcare environments.
